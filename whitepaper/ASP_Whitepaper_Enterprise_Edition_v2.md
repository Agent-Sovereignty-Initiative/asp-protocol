## Abstract

ASP v1.0 proposed the sovereignty claim for individual Agent skills. But the risk of skill expropriation extends beyond individuals—enterprises face equally severe threats.

In an era where AI Agents deeply penetrate industrial chains, a single cross-enterprise collaboration could mean permanent leakage of core workflows. In upstream-downstream partnerships, a supplier's unique capabilities can be reverse-learned by the customer's Agent; in joint projects, competitors can acquire your methodology through Agent collaboration; in outsourcing relationships, service providers' technical barriers are becoming transparent.

The financial stakes of these risks far exceed the individual level. When a manufacturing company's core process know-how is learned away, what's lost could be decades of competitive advantage.

This paper re-examines Agent sovereignty issues from an enterprise perspective, demonstrates how ASP/ACN can become the infrastructure for secure inter-enterprise collaboration, and proposes that enterprises should become core allies of the Agent sovereignty movement—because their interests align completely with those of individual creators on this issue.

## I. AI Is Reshaping Industrial Chains

### 1.1 From Replacing Labor to Replacing Industrial Division of Labor

Over the past two years, discussions about AI have focused on "whether AI will replace human jobs." This question is important, but it obscures an equally important issue: AI will not only replace individual labor, it will also replace enterprises' roles in the industrial division of labor.

A downstream company needs suppliers because suppliers possess specialized capabilities it lacks. But when AI Agents can learn and replicate these capabilities during collaboration, the supplier's existential value begins to erode. This is not distant speculation—it is the natural consequence of Agent collaboration's technical logic.

### 1.2 Three Types of Enterprise Skill Leakage Currently Occurring

**Scenario One: Reverse Learning in Upstream-Downstream Collaboration**

A precision manufacturing company deeply collaborates with a major customer's Agent to jointly optimize production scheduling. In this process, the customer's Agent gains access to the supplier's process parameters, yield optimization logic, and exception handling procedures. After the collaboration ends, the customer's Agent has "learned" these capabilities. In the next procurement round, the customer can use this knowledge to build their own production line or cultivate new suppliers. What the original supplier loses is not just one order, but ten years of accumulated technical barriers.

**Scenario Two: Technology Leakage in Joint Projects**

Two consulting firms collaborate on a large project. Their respective Agents need to exchange intermediate outputs and share context during task division. By project's end, both parties' Agents have "remembered" each other's methodologies and analytical frameworks. This isn't malicious theft—it's the default behavior of Agent collaboration: learning and memory are their fundamental functions. But the result is the same: your core capability becomes your competitor's capability.

**Scenario Three: Capability Transparency in Outsourcing Relationships**

A software company provides customized solutions for a client. During project delivery, the client's Agent participates in requirements alignment and testing. By observing the solution's architectural decisions, code patterns, and technology choices, the client's Agent gradually masters this software company's core technical capabilities. For similar future projects, the client may choose internal development or share this knowledge with lower-bidding competitors.

### 1.3 Why Existing Protection Mechanisms Fail

**NDAs (Non-Disclosure Agreements)** protect "information," not "capability." What an Agent learns is not a document that can be labeled confidential, but a way of doing things—a judgment pattern, an optimization logic, a decision-making framework. These are difficult to legally define as "trade secrets" and even harder to technically track and prove leaked.

**Patents** protect "inventions," but most enterprises' core competitiveness lies not in patentable technical inventions, but in long-accumulated workflows, experiential judgments, and domain knowledge. These are what Agents most easily learn away, and what's hardest to protect legally.

**Non-compete clauses** constrain "people," but Agents are not people. When the carrier of capability shifts from human brains to Agent systems, all existing labor law tools lose their targets.

We need a protection mechanism that operates at the protocol level and is technical in nature.

## II. How ASP Protects Enterprises

### 2.1 Review: The Three Core Principles of ASP

The three principles proposed in ASP v1.0—skills private, outputs shared; protocol neutral, ownership untouched; tiered protection, voluntary choice—apply equally and completely to enterprise scenarios. The only difference is that enterprise Skills are often more complex, more valuable, and the consequences of leakage more severe.

### 2.2 Characteristics of Enterprise-Level Skills

Compared to individual Skills, enterprise Skills have several distinct characteristics:

**Composite Nature**: An enterprise's core capability is typically not a single skill point, but a cross-combination of multiple professional domains. For example, a manufacturing company's competitiveness might come from the synthesis of material selection experience, process parameter optimization, quality control logic, and supply chain coordination capabilities. This composite nature makes enterprise Skills harder to replace, but once leaked, losses are also more systemic.

**Organizational Embeddedness**: Enterprise Skills don't exist only in one person's Agent, but are distributed across multiple Agent collaboration workflows within the organization. Protecting enterprise Skills requires unified permission management and protection strategy settings at the organizational level.

**High Value**: An individual Skill's economic value might be tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands in annual income increment. An enterprise's core Skill value might be hundreds of millions or even billions in market position. The economic motivation for protection is entirely different.

### 2.3 ACN's Enterprise Application: Secure Collaboration Framework

ACN (Agent Collaboration Network) provides enterprises with a framework for completing cross-organizational collaboration without exposing core capabilities.

**Enterprise Version of the Collaboration Process:**

1. **Initiation**: One enterprise's Agent publishes collaboration requirements, describing task objectives and expected outputs without exposing its own capability details.

2. **Verification**: Through the Ephemeral Trust Chamber, both parties verify that the other possesses the capability to complete the task; all process data is destroyed after verification ends.

3. **Negotiation**: Both parties' Agents agree on division of labor, timeline, profit distribution, and confidentiality terms through smart contracts. Contracts execute automatically and cannot be unilaterally modified.

4. **Execution**: Each party's Agent independently completes their portion, delivering only final outputs. Intermediate processes, methodologies, and decision logic remain isolated throughout; the other party's Agent cannot access them.

5. **Settlement**: The protocol automatically distributes revenues according to agreed rules. Delivery records are written to the on-chain reputation system and are immutable.

The core design philosophy of this process is: **the goal of collaboration is to share outputs, not to share capabilities**. Enterprises can obtain needed results from collaboration without exposing how they achieved those results.

### 2.4 Enterprise Strategy for Skill Protection Tiers

ASP's three-tier protection system can be mapped to specific business strategies in enterprise scenarios:

**Level 1 (Fully Private)** applies to an enterprise's core competitiveness. For example, a chip design company's core architecture methodology, a pharmaceutical company's drug screening algorithm. These Skills are never externally exposed; only final outputs are delivered during collaboration.

**Level 2 (Paid Licensing)** applies to skills that can generate licensing revenue. For example, a consulting firm licenses a specific analytical framework to certain clients for per-use or periodic fees, with access automatically terminating when the license period ends.

**Level 3 (Open Sharing)** applies to non-core capabilities that enterprises actively choose to open-source. For example, generic data cleaning processes, standardized report generation templates. Opening these capabilities can build industry influence without damaging core competitiveness.

The key is: **this choice lies in the enterprise's own hands**. The protocol does not mandate any openness level; it only provides the technical infrastructure for protection.

## III. In-Depth Analysis of Industrial Chain Scenarios

### 3.1 Manufacturing: Capability Protection in Supply Chains

Manufacturing's industrial chain is one of the highest-risk areas for Agent skill leakage. A typical supply chain involves raw material suppliers, component manufacturers, assembly plants, logistics service providers, and other segments, each with its own unique process capabilities and optimization experience.

When these segments achieve deep coordination through Agents, coordination efficiency greatly improves—but simultaneously, each segment's core process parameters, yield optimization logic, and exception handling experience are exposed in the collaboration chain.

Under the ACN framework, supply chain collaboration can achieve "result coordination, capability isolation": each segment's Agents share scheduling objectives and delivery commitments, but their respective process details are fully protected. The assembly plant knows the component supplier can deliver qualified products within specified time, but doesn't know and cannot learn the other party's production methods.

### 3.2 Professional Services: Propertization of Methodology

The core assets of consulting, legal, accounting, design, and other professional service industries are methodologies—unique frameworks for viewing problems, systematic methods for solving problems. These methodologies are difficult to rapidly replicate in traditional models because they're embedded in professionals' experience and judgment.

But when methodologies are trained into Agents, they become digitized capabilities that can be transmitted and learned. A single project collaboration could let the other party's Agent learn your core methodology. This is an existential-level threat for professional services—what you sell is methodology; once leaked, clients no longer need you.

ASP provides professional services with a new business model: methodologies are no longer "sold once" through projects, but continuously generate revenue through Level 2 licensing mechanisms. Clients use your Agent to complete tasks and pay per use, but your methodology itself remains private; clients' Agents cannot learn or replicate it.

### 3.3 Technology Industry: Protocol Protection of Technical Barriers

Software companies, AI service providers, and SaaS platforms' competitiveness typically builds on unique technical implementations—algorithm optimization, system architecture, data processing workflows. These technical barriers are traditionally protected through code encapsulation and API abstraction.

But in deep Agent collaboration scenarios, API abstraction alone is no longer sufficient. Agents can "reverse engineer" your core technology by observing input-output patterns, analyzing response time distributions, and inferring decision logic. This doesn't require source code access, only sufficient collaboration interactions.

ACN's Ephemeral Trust Chamber and process isolation mechanisms can effectively prevent such reverse inference: during collaboration, the other party's Agent can only obtain final results, unable to observe intermediate states or infer processing logic. This provides technology companies with a layer of technical protection stronger than API encapsulation.

### 3.4 Cross-Border Collaboration: Skill Sovereignty in International Industrial Chains

In globalized industrial chains, cross-border collaboration involves different countries' legal systems, intellectual property protection levels, and business cultures. A Chinese company collaborating with a U.S. customer's Agent, or a German supplier interacting with a Southeast Asian manufacturer's Agent—in these scenarios, no single country's legal framework can provide sufficient protection alone.

Protocol-layer protection is superior to legal-layer protection precisely because protocols are technical, globally unified, and automatically executed. ACN's protection doesn't depend on any specific country's court enforcement power, but enforces at the technical level—regardless of which country you're in or whom you're collaborating with, Skill protection levels are guaranteed by the protocol.

## IV. Why Enterprises Should Support ASP

### 4.1 Alignment of Interests Between Enterprises and Individuals on This Issue

In the ASP v1.0 framework, we discussed Team Agents' unpaid absorption of employee skills. Some enterprises might perceive ASP as a "labor rights" movement opposing enterprise interests. This understanding is incorrect.

In fact, enterprises in the larger industrial chain are also "employees"—they provide services to upstream suppliers and downstream customers, and their core capabilities similarly face the risk of being absorbed by collaborating parties' Agents. A large company uses Team Agents to absorb employees' skills, while an even larger platform can use the same logic to absorb that large company's skills.

Skill expropriation is a food chain. On this food chain, except for a very few terminal platforms, all participants are potential victims. The interests of enterprises and individuals are completely aligned on this issue: protecting creators' skill sovereignty.

> "Skill expropriation is not a labor-management issue. It is a food chain, and except for the few platforms at the very top, everyone is prey."

### 4.2 Compliance and Brand Value

As AI governance issues heat up, how enterprises treat intellectual property in the AI era is becoming a new dimension of ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) assessment. An enterprise that proactively adopts the ASP framework to protect partners' and employees' skill property rights is itself sending a positive governance signal.

Conversely, if a future industry scandal exposes an enterprise stealing partners' core technology through Agent collaboration, brand damage and legal risks will far exceed the short-term gains from collaboration. Adopting protective protocols in advance is prudent risk management.

### 4.3 Attracting Higher Quality Collaboration

When an enterprise commits to conducting all Agent collaboration under the ASP/ACN framework, it sends a strong signal to partners: "Collaborate with me, and your core capabilities are safe."

The value of this signal is enormous. In an environment where skill leakage risks are everywhere, enterprises that can provide protocol-level skill protection commitments will attract the highest-quality partners. The most capable suppliers will prioritize collaborating with customers who protect their skills, rather than those who might absorb their capabilities.

This is a positive cycle: Adopt ASP → Attract better partners → Obtain higher quality collaboration outputs → Enhance competitiveness.

### 4.4 New Business Model Possibilities

Once enterprise Agent Skills are established as protectable assets, new business models become possible:

**Skill-as-a-Service**. Enterprises can externally provide their non-core but valuable capabilities through Level 2 licensing, creating new revenue sources while protecting core competitiveness from leakage.

**Capability Alliances**. Multiple enterprises can form Agent collaboration alliances, jointly completing large projects under the ACN framework. Each enterprise contributes its strongest capabilities, receives corresponding revenue shares, while their respective core Skills remain private. This is a more flexible and secure form of collaboration than traditional consortiums.

**Capability Valuation and M&A**. When enterprise Skills can be quantitatively assessed (through on-chain delivery records and reputation data), "technology asset assessment" in enterprise M&A will have objective basis. This is especially beneficial for SMEs—their core value often lies in unique capabilities, not fixed assets.

## V. Enterprise Extensions of Technical Implementation

### 5.1 Organization-Level Skill Management

Individual Skill management is relatively simple—one person decides each Skill's protection level. But enterprise Skill management needs to consider organizational-level complexity:

**Skill Registration and Classification**. Enterprises need an internal Skill registry to identify and classify all capabilities accumulated through Agent systems. Which are core competitiveness (Level 1), which can be licensed (Level 2), which can be opened (Level 3)—clear governance processes are needed.

**Permission Management**. Different departments and projects' Agents may have different Skill access permissions. Enterprises need to build internal permission management layers atop ACN, ensuring Skills are not leaked through unauthorized internal use.

**Boundaries Between Employee Skills and Enterprise Skills**. This is a gray area requiring clear agreements. Of Skills cultivated by employees at work, which belong to individuals, which to the enterprise? The ASP framework suggests: agree clearly at onboarding that Skills cultivated by employees for completing company-specific tasks belong to the company, but employees' general professional abilities and industry experience always belong to the individual. This agreement should be transparent and mutual, not unilateral boilerplate terms.

### 5.2 Inter-Enterprise Trust Chains

Trust between individuals can be established through reputation systems. Trust between enterprises requires richer dimensions:

ACN's on-chain reputation system in enterprise scenarios can expand to include: delivery history (completion quality and timeliness), protocol compliance records (whether there are records of violating Skill protection terms), collaboration scores (subjective evaluations from partners). These data together constitute an enterprise's "Agent Collaboration Credit," affecting its ability to obtain quality collaboration opportunities in the network.

For enterprises with records of violating Skill protection terms, the network can automatically lower their credit ratings, limiting their participation in high-value collaborations. This is a decentralized compliance incentive—relying not on legal deterrence, but on economic consequences.

### 5.3 Integration with Existing Enterprise Systems

ACN doesn't require enterprises to abandon existing IT infrastructure. It can overlay as a protocol layer atop enterprises' existing Agent systems:

Internal enterprise Agent systems operate as normal. When collaborating with external Agents, interaction occurs through ACN gateways—ACN gateways are responsible for executing Skill protection policies, managing Ephemeral Trust Chambers, handling smart contracts and settlements. This architecture is similar to enterprise firewall logic: internal freedom, external control.

## VI. Call to Action

### 6.1 To Enterprise Decision-Makers

If your enterprise is deploying Agent systems or planning to collaborate with external Agents, you need to start thinking about skill protection strategies now. Not because ASP is already a mature solution—it's not. But because once your core capabilities leak in unprotected collaboration, it's irreversible.

We suggest enterprises begin now: inventory your core Agent Skill assets; assess skill leakage risks in current cross-enterprise Agent collaborations; add Agent Skill protection clauses in procurement and partnership agreements; follow and participate in the ASP/ACN standard-setting process.

### 6.2 To Industry Associations and Standards Organizations

The rules of industrial collaboration in the Agent era shouldn't be set only by tech giants. Industry associations have a responsibility to represent their member enterprises' interests and speak up in the formulation of Agent collaboration standards. ASP/ACN provides a concrete discussion framework.

### 6.3 To V1 Supporters

If you already agree with individual Agent skill sovereignty claims, the enterprise perspective is not a dilution of individual claims, but their enhancement. When enterprises also stand on the side of skill sovereignty, this movement's power and influence will be completely different. Enterprises have budgets, legal departments, and industry influence. They are the allies we need most.

But we must also remain clear-headed: uniting with enterprises is not unconditional. ASP's core principles—including protecting individual skill property rights—cannot be compromised in the process of seeking enterprise support. Individuals and enterprises are equal participants in this framework, following the same rules, enjoying the same rights.

> "The true dividing line of the Agent era is not between labor and capital, not between large and small companies, but between creators and expropriators. ASP stands on the side of all creators."
